Mayor Eric Adams and DOT Commissioner Ydanis Rodriguez have upgraded more than 1,200 intersections across New York City for safety. These improvements include installing raised crosswalks, leading pedestrian intervals, shortening crossings, or other interventions. What do these interventions look like?

Using Open Data, we've mapped:

Raised crosswalks. Mayor Adams promised to install 100 raised crosswalks in 2022.

Turn calming interventions. DOT committed to install turn calming elements at 100 intersections in 2022.

Signal timing only upgrades, including leading pedestrian intervals. No new physical infrastructure was installed.

Vision Zero priority intersection upgrades. These are intersections known to be dangerous to the City.

About the data

The Intersection Upgrades Tracker compiles changes from NYC Open Data's Raised Crosswalks, Leading Pedestrian Interval Signals (LPIs), Turn Traffic Calming, and Traffic Signal and All-Way Stop Study Requests datasets. The city has not provided a dataset that specifies bike corrals daylighting intersections. (The Bicycle Parking dataset includes bike corrals, but not this information.) Additionally, there are some intersection changes that NYC DOT could implement that do not show up in any Open Data datasets in any form, including adding Barnes Dances (or any other non-LPI signal changes), pedestrian islands, and any bulb-outs that might not be classified as turn calming. Our combined dataset is available on GitHub.

What is a protected bike lane?

Raised Crosswalks

Raised crosswalks put crosswalks at the same height as sidewalks. Raised crosswalks increase visibility, improve accessibility, and serve as speed bumps that slow drivers.

Leading Pedestrian Interval

These show a walk sign for pedestrians before a green light to car traffic, improving visibility by allowing pedestrians to begin crossing the street before cars make turns across the crosswalk.

Bike Corral

Bike corrals serve "daylight" intersections, increasing intersection visibility for drivers, bike riders, and pedestrians. Their placement also helps prevent drivers from taking turns too quickly.

Left Turn Calming

DOT places rubber curbs, flexible plastic posts, or bollards along the path of the turn to encourage drivers to follow the intended path for the turn and slow drivers down.

Traffic Signal

Traffic signals give pedestrians a specific time to cross an intersection without waiting for a gap in traffic and clarify when drivers, cyclists, and pedestrians should proceed.

All-Way Stop

All-way stops allow pedestrians to cross an intersection without waiting for a gap in traffic and make crossing less chaotic for drivers and cyclists.
